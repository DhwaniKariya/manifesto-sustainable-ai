Manifesto
for Sustainable AI

Version 0.3 · Signed · ICAM BIP Toulouse · 2 July 2026

Artificial intelligence offers real benefits for Europe, but its growth must not come at the cost of the environment, people's rights, or public trust. We, the undersigned stakeholders, agree the principles and commitments below to make AI in Europe sustainable, fair, and trustworthy — leaving the world better for the next generation than we found it.

Foundation

Shared Principles

Seven values every signatory commits to uphold, regardless of sector.

01
Growth

Europe's innovation and competitiveness are worth defending.
02
Technology

AI built and used responsibly, safely, and openly where possible.
03
Environment

AI's energy, water, and material footprint measured honestly and reduced.
04
People

Workers, users, and citizens protected; the benefits shared.
05
Fair Governance

Rules proportionate, transparent, and evidence-based.
06
Inclusion

AI must be accessible, equitable, and beneficial across all communities.
07
Public Trust

Transparency, explainability, and independent verification are essential.
In Detail

What Each Principle Means

Select any numbered tile above to jump to its full explanation.

01
Growth

EU AI sovereignty is not just an economic goal — it is a strategic necessity. When every frontier model is trained and served outside Europe, the continent's intelligence infrastructure sits on foreign soil. Growth here means building AI that is genuinely European in origin: trained on EU infrastructure, governed by EU law, and available to EU researchers and businesses on competitive terms. The five signatory groups agree that competitiveness and responsibility are not opposites — the most trusted AI systems will win the long game.

02
Technology

Responsible technology means deliberate choices at every stage: how models are trained, documented, and deployed; what risks are assessed and disclosed; who can audit the system. Openness — where safety permits — widens scrutiny and reduces the concentration of AI power in a handful of actors. The GPAI Code of Practice, system cards, and harmonised EU standards give every sector a shared vocabulary for what "responsible" actually means, rather than leaving it as a marketing claim.

03
Environment

The numbers are real: data centres projected to consume 945 TWh globally by 2030 — roughly double 2024 levels. Water consumption rising to 1,200 billion litres per year. A single hyperscaler site occupying 190 hectares. 62 million tonnes of e-waste in 2022, only 20% properly recycled. Measuring honestly is the prerequisite for reducing systematically. The EU methodology — PUE, WUE, and land use — creates a common baseline. Without it, every group reports differently and no progress is verifiable.

04
People

AI is not a neutral tool. It makes decisions about credit, insurance, employment, and content at scales no human review process can match. The People principle demands that those affected — workers displaced by automation, users subject to algorithmic decisions, communities hosting AI infrastructure — have recourse, explanation, and a share of the value AI creates. Reskilling funds, human oversight for high-stakes decisions, and transparency requirements are the mechanisms; protecting people is the purpose.

05
Fair Governance

Good governance is harder than either extreme — it is not the absence of rules, and it is not the accumulation of them. The EU AI Act's risk-tier structure is the right architecture: lighter for low-risk tools, stricter for high-impact systems, with harmonised standards and regulatory sandboxes to smooth the path for smaller actors. Transparency about regulatory intentions and realistic timelines is itself a form of fairness. The review milestone in 2028 (aligned to Art. 40) gives every sector a known checkpoint — not perpetual uncertainty.

06
Inclusion

AI must not replicate or deepen existing inequalities. Accessibility means that the benefits — better healthcare diagnostics, more efficient public services, educational tools — reach rural communities, low-income households, people with disabilities, and underserved populations, not just those who can already afford the best of everything. Equitable deployment requires that AI systems are designed and tested across diverse demographic groups. The digital divide is a policy choice: this manifesto requires every signatory sector to name the communities they are reaching, not only the markets they are serving.

07
Public Trust

Trust in AI cannot be assumed — it must be earned and continually renewed. Transparency means users know when they are interacting with an AI, how it made a decision, and what data it used. Explainability means that affected individuals can understand and challenge outcomes. Independent verification means that claims about safety, environmental impact, and fairness are checked by parties with no commercial interest in the result. Without these three elements, public trust is marketing copy. With them, it becomes the foundation on which everything else in this manifesto rests.

Agreed Commitments

Commitments by Sector

What each of the seven groups has agreed and signed. Every point below is part of the final manifesto.

🧠

AI Model Providers

  • 01
    Adhere to the GPAI Code of Practice (Transparency, Copyright, Safety & Security) as the recognised compliance route; voluntary adherence provides a presumption of conformity until harmonised European standards are published.
    Source: European Commission — GPAI Code of Practice
  • 02
    Document known or estimated training energy and report a representative inference figure (e.g. energy per standard response). Comparable cross-provider reporting follows a single agreed EU measurement methodology before it becomes binding.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 53 & Annex XI
  • 03
    Publish a system card per model release covering capabilities, evaluations, limitations and risk assessment; maintain a public list of safety commitments.
  • 04
    Requirements apply to all providers serving the EU market (level playing field); reporting templates standardised and free; obligations scale with provider size (SME / small mid-cap relief).
    Source: European Parliament — Digital Omnibus on AI
🏭

Data Centre Companies

  • 01
    Publish annually their PUE/WUE under the EU methodology, with high-level improvement targets; SMEs publish aggregated KPIs.
    Source: EED (EU) 2023/1791 + Delegated Reg (EU) 2024/1364
  • 02
    Procure clean energy; reuse/recirculate water; recover waste heat where technically, economically and locally feasible.
  • 03
    In water-stressed locations, disclose cooling-water source and drought safeguards, and do not deploy water-intensive cooling that materially competes with household, agricultural or ecological water needs.
  • 04
    Support a phased EU data-centre sustainability rating scheme using a comparable EU methodology and, once adopted, publish the applicable rating and legally required public KPIs.
    Source: European Commission — EU-wide sustainability rating scheme
  • 05
    Genuine trade secrets (security architecture, pricing, supplier contracts, granular telemetry) remain confidential to competent authorities only; this is a voluntary sector commitment.
    Source: Commission Delegated Reg (EU) 2024/1364
⚙️

AI Engineers

  • 01
    Apply energy-efficient development and documentation practices, anticipating AI Act harmonised standards on resource use.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 40
  • 02
    Maintain professional accountability: document design choices, uphold cross-border compliance awareness, and conduct a user-impact review before deployment.
  • 03
    Support workforce transition and reskilling with employers, governments and educators; anchored to the EU Pact for Skills.
    Source: European Commission — EU Pact for Skills
🏢

Companies Using AI (Deployers)

  • 01
    Human oversight for every high-risk decision (credit, insurance, claims, employment): no loan, claim, benefit or price decided by algorithm alone.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 14 — human oversight
  • 02
    Transparency and a right to explanation when AI sets a price or makes a decision; no pricing or underwriting on protected or sensitive personal data.
    Source: GDPR, Art. 22 — automated decisions
  • 03
    Meet AI Act transparency duties: disclose when a user is interacting with a chatbot; label AI-generated public content unless a human editor has reviewed it and taken legal responsibility.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 50 — transparency
  • 04
    Make AI-content detection interoperable (e.g. a public API or an embedded signpost) so third-party platforms can verify whether content is AI-generated.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 50 — transparency
  • 05
    Categorise business AI tools into risk tiers (low-risk / high-risk / prohibited) and apply the operational rules for each tier.
  • 06
    Align with the EU AI Act and GDPR; adopt voluntary management standards (ISO/IEC 42001) and risk frameworks (NIST AI RMF) as good practice.
    Source: ISO/IEC 42001 · NIST AI RMF 1.0
  • 07
    Provide worker reskilling whenever AI changes a role, conditional on proportionate rules (regulatory sandboxes; SME relief and the deferred high-risk timeline).
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 57 — regulatory sandboxes
  • 08
    Pair each use case with a stated benefit and a managed risk; submit evidence on which rules help or hinder adoption.
🌍

Environmentalists

  • 01
    Provide verified, sourced footprint metrics (energy, water, land, waste) to inform proportionate limits.
    Source: IEA — Energy and AI, 2025
  • 02
    Ground obligations in existing EU law (EED + Delegated Regulation); support facility-level environmental-KPI transparency with redactions limited to genuine trade secrets.
    Source: EED (EU) 2023/1791 + Delegated Reg (EU) 2024/1364
  • 03
    Recommend (not mandate) that Member States lower the ≥500 kW reporting threshold, and that data centres additionally report a water-reduction strategy, territorial-impact analysis and employment data as EU-wide best practice.
  • 04
    Environmental scrutiny triggers on facility footprint (EED ≥500 kW, or lower national threshold), not on the AI Act model-compute threshold; Art. 55 is reserved for model-level systemic risk.
    Source: EU AI Act, Art. 55 — systemic risk
  • 05
    Take part in independent monitoring and verification of environmental claims alongside Journalists.
⚖️

EU Regulators

  • 01
    Provide proportionate, predictable rules aligned to AI Act risk tiers and GDPR, with guidance, regulatory sandboxes and realistic timelines (including the deferred high-risk dates).
    Source: European Parliament — Digital Omnibus on AI
  • 02
    Provide standardised, free reporting templates; scale obligations to actor size.
  • 03
    Advocate that per-facility environmental-performance KPIs be made public (via amendment to the Delegated Regulation), while protecting genuine trade secrets.
    Source: Commission Delegated Reg (EU) 2024/1364
  • 04
    Convene and chair the multi-stakeholder review council; mediate fairly; act as final arbiter on unresolved points.
📰

Journalists — Coordinators & Co-signatories

  • 01
    Verify all claims and figures against primary sources before publication, and issue corrections where needed.
  • 02
    Be transparent about sources and methods; report each group's position fairly and without bias.
  • 03
    Maintain the shared evidence repository and publish the agreed final manifesto in the public interest.
Legal Framework

Regulatory Backbone

The EU legal foundation underpinning every commitment in this manifesto.

Principle 1
Proportionate Alignment

Align obligations with EU AI Act risk tiers and GDPR; lighter for SMEs and small mid-caps, stricter for high-impact actors.

Source: EU AI Act Implementation Timeline — ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu · GDPR, Art. 5 — eur-lex.europa.eu

Principle 2
Sustainability Transparency

Standard disclosure built on Annex XI energy documentation and EED data-centre energy/water reporting; not diluted by the high-risk deferral.

Source: EU AI Act, Art. 53 & Annex XI — artificialintelligenceact.eu · EED (EU) 2023/1791 — energy.ec.europa.eu

Principle 3
Voluntary-First

GPAI Code of Practice and Art. 40 harmonised standards as the low-friction compliance route — can become binding if voluntary effort falls short.

Source: GPAI Code of Practice — digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu · EU AI Act, Art. 40 — artificialintelligenceact.eu

Principle 4
Incentives

Tie permits and public support to verified sustainability and safety commitments, leaning on the forthcoming EU data-centre rating scheme.

Source: European Commission — EU-wide data-centre sustainability rating scheme — energy.ec.europa.eu

Principle 5
Accountability

Independent verification by Environmentalists and Journalists against published KPIs; review milestone aligned to the Art. 40 report due 2 August 2028.

Source: EU AI Act, Art. 40 — artificialintelligenceact.eu

How We Verify

Measurement, Transparency & Accountability

The metrics, cadence, and verification process that make these commitments real.

What We Measure

Energy and water performance via the EU methodology — PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) and WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness) — reported at facility level to the EU database and published at the agreed level of granularity.

For AI models: training energy plus a representative inference figure (energy per standard response). Measurement method under active discussion.

Cadence

Annual reporting to the EU database. Rating-scheme labels once formally adopted by the Commission.

Who Reports

Reporting obligations trigger on the EED facility threshold (≥500 kW installed IT power, or a lower nationally transposed threshold). Obligations scale with actor size and AI Act risk tier — SME relief built in.

Who Verifies

Independent check by Environmentalists and Journalists against published KPIs. The scope of facility-level publication remains an open point under negotiation, with a formal review milestone aligned to the Art. 40 report due August 2028.

Not yet agreed — next phase of work

Future Points to Add

These points were proposed during the debate but are not included in the signed manifesto. They are the agenda for the next round of negotiations. This section is shown on the website for transparency and is excluded from the downloadable PDF.

🧠 AI Model Providers

  • Establish a joint review council — AI model providers, EU regulators, data centres and AI-using market leaders — to develop and deploy safer and greener models through mutual investment and commitment.
  • Pursue a regulator–provider partnership to keep regulation in step with AI development pace and strengthen Europe against foreign hyperscalers.

🏭 Data Centre Companies

  • Annual independent verification of environmental KPIs (PUE, WUE, water safeguards) by Journalists and Environmentalists — to ensure accuracy and prevent greenwashing. (Scope of facility-level publication vs. confidentiality also remains under negotiation.)

⚙️ AI Engineers

  • Document accessibility considerations as part of standard design documentation (e.g., usability for disabled users).
  • Participate in annual public trust surveys to measure societal confidence in AI systems.

🏢 Companies Using AI

  • Companies must ensure that AI benefits reach rural, low-income, and underserved communities — supporting Europe's digital inclusion goals.

🌍 Environmentalists

  • Add land use and water-stress-zone classification as a fifth reporting dimension to the rating scheme, ideally before its first labels — treat as phase 2 anchored to an existing water-stress index.
  • Publish a yearly report summarising AI's environmental footprint across Europe (energy, water, land impact, waste) — jointly verified with Journalists.

⚖️ EU Regulators

  • Include a clear timeline summarising when different obligations under the EU AI Act become enforceable.
  • Publish an annual briefing summarising progress on AI governance, enforcement, and standardisation.
  • Categorise the priority of data being used by service providers.

📰 Journalists

  • Join the multi-stakeholder review council as non-voting observers, ensuring transparency and public accountability in discussions about AI safety, sustainability, and competitiveness.
  • Publish quarterly summaries of sustainability, safety, and governance developments across all stakeholder groups.

📋 Regulatory Backbone

  • Documentation requirement — all AI model providers and data centre operators publish standardised annual documentation reports, increasing transparency and building public trust.

Open Negotiation Points — 9 Unresolved Items

Open Point 1
Facility-Level Environmental Disclosure

Environmentalists vs Data Centres: how much per-facility KPI data becomes public. Regulator lean: energy, water, PUE/WUE and cooling safeguards public; security, pricing, contracts and granular telemetry remain confidential.

Open Point 2
Comparable Energy-Reporting Methodology

Adopt a single EU measurement standard before "comparable" cross-provider reporting becomes binding. Proposed basis: Mistral's lifecycle indicators; apply to all providers serving the EU market.

Open Point 3
Multi-Stakeholder Review Council

At AI Model Providers' request: a joint council of providers, EU regulators, data centres and AI-using leaders. Mandate, membership (proposed: add environmentalists) and regulator chair to be agreed.

Open Point 4
Rating-Scheme Land-Use / Water-Stress Dimension

Environmentalists ask for a fifth KPI category before the rating scheme's first labels (expected August 2027). No harmonised EU land-use dataset exists — treat as phase 2.

Open Point 5
Proportionality, SME Relief & Free Templates

Standardise free reporting templates and scale obligations to actor size so compliance cost does not fall disproportionately on EU startups and SMEs.

Open Point 6
Regulatory Pace & Competitiveness Partnership

A regulator–provider partnership to update rules at AI development pace. Regulator lean: support competitiveness, faster standardisation, and a level playing field — not reduced scrutiny.

Open Point 7
Tax Rebates & Incentives for Green AI

Tax rebates or other incentives for companies and AI model providers using verified greener AI approaches — proposed by data centres and EU regulators.

Open Point 8
Reskilling Funds & R&D Startup Funding

Dedicated public funding for worker reskilling programmes and R&D support for AI startups. Scope and delivery mechanism to be agreed between regulators and deployers.

Open Point 9
Independent Verification of All Sustainability Claims

All sustainability, safety, and transparency claims made by AI model providers and data centres will undergo independent verification by Journalists and Environmentalists.

Manifesto
for Sustainable AI

Version 0.2 · Agreed by consensus · ICAM BIP 2026

Artificial intelligence offers real benefits for Europe, but its growth must not come at the cost of the environment, people's rights, or public trust. We, the undersigned stakeholders, agree the principles and commitments below to make AI in Europe sustainable, fair, and trustworthy — leaving the world better for the next generation than we found it.

In Detail

What Each Principle Means

Click any numbered tile above to jump to its full explanation.

01
Growth

EU AI sovereignty is not just an economic goal — it is a strategic necessity. When every frontier model is trained and served outside Europe, the continent's intelligence infrastructure sits on foreign soil. Growth here means building AI that is genuinely European in origin: trained on EU infrastructure, governed by EU law, and available to EU researchers and businesses on competitive terms. The five signatory groups agree that competitiveness and responsibility are not opposites — the most trusted AI systems will win the long game.

02
Technology

Responsible technology means deliberate choices at every stage: how models are trained, documented, and deployed; what risks are assessed and disclosed; who can audit the system. Openness — where safety permits — widens scrutiny and reduces the concentration of AI power in a handful of actors. The GPAI Code of Practice, system cards, and harmonised EU standards give every sector a shared vocabulary for what "responsible" actually means, rather than leaving it as a marketing claim.

03
Environment

The numbers are real: data centres projected to consume 945 TWh globally by 2030 — roughly double 2024 levels. Water consumption rising to 1,200 billion litres per year. A single hyperscaler site occupying 190 hectares. 62 million tonnes of e-waste in 2022, only 20% properly recycled. Measuring honestly is the prerequisite for reducing systematically. The EU methodology — PUE, WUE, and land use — creates a common baseline. Without it, every group reports differently and no progress is verifiable.

04
People

AI is not a neutral tool. It makes decisions about credit, insurance, employment, and content at scales no human review process can match. The People principle demands that those affected — workers displaced by automation, users subject to algorithmic decisions, communities hosting AI infrastructure — have recourse, explanation, and a share of the value AI creates. Reskilling funds, human oversight for high-stakes decisions, and transparency requirements are the mechanisms; protecting people is the purpose.

05
Fair Governance

Good governance is harder than either extreme — it is not the absence of rules, and it is not the accumulation of them. The EU AI Act's risk-tier structure is the right architecture: lighter for low-risk tools, stricter for high-impact systems, with harmonised standards and regulatory sandboxes to smooth the path for smaller actors. Transparency about regulatory intentions and realistic timelines is itself a form of fairness. The review milestone in 2028 (aligned to Art. 40) gives every sector a known checkpoint — not perpetual uncertainty.

Legal Framework

Regulatory Backbone

The EU legal foundation underpinning every commitment in this manifesto.

Principle 1
Proportionate Alignment

Align obligations with EU AI Act risk tiers and GDPR; lighter for SMEs and small mid-caps, stricter for high-impact actors.

Source: EU AI Act Implementation Timeline — ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu · GDPR, Art. 5 — eur-lex.europa.eu

Principle 2
Sustainability Transparency

Standard disclosure built on Annex XI energy documentation and EED data-centre energy/water reporting; not diluted by the high-risk deferral.

Source: EU AI Act, Art. 53 & Annex XI — artificialintelligenceact.eu · EED (EU) 2023/1791 — energy.ec.europa.eu

Principle 3
Voluntary-First

GPAI Code of Practice and Art. 40 harmonised standards as the low-friction compliance route — can become binding if voluntary effort falls short.

Source: GPAI Code of Practice — digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu · EU AI Act, Art. 40 — artificialintelligenceact.eu

Principle 4
Incentives

Tie permits and public support to verified sustainability and safety commitments, leaning on the forthcoming EU data-centre rating scheme.

Source: European Commission — EU-wide data-centre sustainability rating scheme — energy.ec.europa.eu

Principle 5
Accountability

Independent verification by Environmentalists and Journalists against published KPIs; review milestone aligned to the Art. 40 report due 2 August 2028.

Source: EU AI Act, Art. 40 — artificialintelligenceact.eu

How We Verify

Measurement, Transparency & Accountability

The metrics, cadence, and verification process that make these commitments real.

What We Measure

Energy and water performance via the EU methodology — PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) and WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness) — reported at facility level to the EU database and published at the agreed level of granularity.

For AI models: training energy plus a representative inference figure (energy per standard response). Measurement method under active discussion.

Cadence

Annual reporting to the EU database. Rating-scheme labels once formally adopted by the Commission.

Who Reports

Reporting obligations trigger on the EED facility threshold (≥500 kW installed IT power, or a lower nationally transposed threshold). Obligations scale with actor size and AI Act risk tier — SME relief built in.

Who Verifies

Independent check by Environmentalists and Journalists against published KPIs. The scope of facility-level publication remains an open point under negotiation, with a formal review milestone aligned to the Art. 40 report due August 2028.
